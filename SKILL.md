# Seeding by Ceding Assessment

A MacKenzie Scott methodology for identifying opportunities where stepping back and ceding control will produce better outcomes than maintaining oversight.

## Purpose

Growth often comes from giving up, not holding on. This skill helps you identify where your control is limiting potential, and where yielding that control would seed greater growth—in people, projects, and organizations.

## Workflow
### Step 1: Map Your Control Points

Where do you currently hold control? Be specific.

Control manifests as:
- **Decision authority** - Final say on choices
- **Information control** - Being the hub through which knowledge flows
- **Resource control** - Controlling access to money, tools, opportunities
- **Process control** - Defining how things must be done
- **Approval gates** - Requiring your sign-off before action
- **Direct involvement** - Being present and active in execution

**Inventory question:** For each control point, ask: "Does this serve the goal, or does it serve my comfort?"

### Step 2: Assess the Cost of Control

What is your control costing?

| Control Cost | How It Manifests |
|--------------|------------------|
| Bottleneck | Decisions wait on you; speed suffers |
| Dependency | Others can't function without you |
| Capability atrophy | Others don't develop skills they'd gain from autonomy |
| Ownership deficit | Others don't feel accountable because you own everything |
| Burnout (yours) | You're overextended maintaining control |
| Disengagement (theirs) | People disengage when they can't exercise judgment |

**Key question:** What would happen if you were unavailable for a month? Would things improve, stay the same, or collapse?

### Step 3: Identify Ceding Opportunities

For each control point, ask: "What if I yielded this?"

**Ceding spectrum:**
- **Full cede** - Remove yourself entirely from this area
- **Graduated cede** - Phase out your involvement over defined time
- **Conditional cede** - Step back unless specific triggers occur
- **Consultative cede** - Available if asked, but not directing

**Selection criteria:**
- Where is someone else closer to the work than you?
- Where are capable people waiting for autonomy?
- Where has your control become routine rather than necessary?
- Where would your absence force growth?

### Step 4: Reframe Your Concerns

Your worries about stepping back may be assets in disguise.

| Your Concern | The Reframe |
|--------------|-------------|
| "They'll make mistakes" | Mistakes are learning opportunities you're currently denying them |
| "Quality will suffer" | They might find approaches better than yours |
| "They'll go in the wrong direction" | Their direction might be more innovative |
| "I'm needed" | Your value may be in what you enable, not what you do |
| "It won't be done my way" | Your way isn't the only good way |
| "Things will fall apart" | Short-term disruption may yield long-term strength |

**The fundamental question:** Is your control producing growth, or preventing it?

### Step 5: Design the Cede

How will you step back? Structure the transition.

**Elements of a good cede:**
- **Clear transfer** - Explicitly name what you're yielding and to whom
- **No backseat driving** - Commit to not second-guessing or hovering
- **Support without control** - Offer yourself as resource, not overseer
- **Defined absence** - Be specific about how you're stepping back
- **Trust communicated** - Tell them why you're trusting them with this

**Avoid the false cede:**
- Delegating but still requiring approval
- Stepping back but checking in constantly
- Giving authority but not information
- Ceding responsibility but not credit

### Step 6: Execute and Observe

Put the cede into practice.

**Actions:**
1. Communicate the cede clearly to all involved
2. Follow through—actually step back
3. Resist the pull to re-engage when discomfort arises
4. Notice what happens (observation, not intervention)
5. Celebrate their successes as theirs, not yours

## Example Application

**Input:**
"I'm leading a product team of 8 people. Every feature decision comes through me. I review every design. I approve every sprint plan. I'm exhausted and my team seems disengaged. Where should I step back?"

**Analysis:**

**Control Point Inventory:**
- Feature decisions (final authority)
- Design reviews (approval gate)
- Sprint plans (approval gate)
- Likely also: priorities, timelines, stakeholder communication

**Cost Assessment:**
- **Bottleneck:** Team waits on you for everything; velocity is limited by your availability
- **Dependency:** No one can move without you; you can never truly be unavailable
- **Capability atrophy:** Your designers aren't developing judgment because you always provide it
- **Ownership deficit:** Team doesn't own outcomes—you do, so they execute but don't invest
- **Burnout:** You're exhausted
- **Disengagement:** They seem disengaged (your observation confirms this)

Your control is costing more than it's contributing.

**Ceding Opportunities (ranked):**
1. **Design reviews** - Full cede to design lead; they're closer to craft than you
2. **Sprint plans** - Full cede to team; they know their capacity and constraints
3. **Feature decisions** - Graduated cede; move to consultative role over 3 months
4. **Stakeholder communication** - Conditional cede; distribute to relevant leads

**Concern Reframes:**
- "Quality will drop" - Your team includes professionals; trust their craft
- "We'll build the wrong thing" - You'll still be in planning conversations; cede execution, not strategy
- "Stakeholders expect me" - This is about your comfort, not stakeholder needs

**Cede Design:**
- Week 1: Announce the change. Explain why. Express trust explicitly.
- Design reviews: "These are now owned by [Design Lead]. I won't be reviewing unless she asks."
- Sprint plans: "The team owns sprint planning. I'll attend to answer questions, not to approve."
- Features: "I'm moving to advisory. Bring me in when you want perspective, not permission."
- Commitment: "I will not second-guess decisions made in areas I've ceded."

**First Action:**
Have an explicit conversation with your design lead tomorrow. Tell her: "I've been holding onto design reviews because of my own discomfort, not because you need me to. Starting now, these are yours. I trust your judgment."

## Core Principle

> "Putting large donors at the center of stories on social progress is a distortion of their role. We are attempting to give away a fortune that was enabled by systems in need of change. The solutions are best designed and implemented by others." — MacKenzie Scott

Your value is not in what you control. It's in what you enable by letting go. Seeding happens through ceding.
